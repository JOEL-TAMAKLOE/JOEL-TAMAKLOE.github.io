Reviving Ride Demand Through Data-Driven Field Strategy: A Yango Experience

By Joel Tamakloe · Published: January 2026 · 5 min read
Data Analysis Business Strategy Field Operations Marketplace Growth Ride-Hailing

This article examines how a data-driven field strategy was used to revive ride demand in Tema after years of declining driver activity on Yango. By combining marketplace data analysis, fare experimentation, and on-the-ground execution, the project reveals how misaligned supply, inactive drivers, and pricing perception can quietly erode platform performance. The experience highlights why sustainable marketplace growth depends not only on dashboards, but on decisions informed by data and validated in the real world.

When a Marketplace Loses Its Balance

Ride-hailing platforms live and die by balance. When riders cannot find cars and drivers cannot find value, trust erodes on both sides of the marketplace. In Tema, Yango faced exactly this challenge. Driver activity had been steadily declining for nearly three years, quietly weakening the system long before it became visible to customers.

The symptoms were clear. Longer estimated times of arrival, lower ride completion rates, and declining rider confidence. What was less obvious was the cause. Despite repeated driver acquisition campaigns, the problem persisted, suggesting that the issue ran deeper than simple supply shortages.

Data creates value only when insights are translated into action.

Diagnosing the Problem Beyond the Obvious

Rather than treating the situation as a driver shortage, this project began with a diagnostic approach grounded in data. Historical trip data, driver activity logs, fare behavior, and geographic demand patterns were analyzed to understand where and why the system was breaking down.

Several patterns emerged quickly:

  • High-demand zones consistently lacked active drivers during peak hours
  • A large percentage of registered drivers were inactive or semi-active
  • Long ETAs discouraged riders, reinforcing a negative feedback loop
  • Previous activation efforts were static and poorly targeted
Key Insight:
Demand was not absent. It was misaligned with where and when drivers were available.

This distinction was critical. The challenge was not attracting new drivers into the system, but understanding why existing drivers had disengaged and why supply consistently failed to appear where demand actually existed.

What the Data Could Not Fully Capture

While quantitative analysis revealed strong patterns, it also exposed an important limitation. Certain factors influencing driver behavior were not fully represented in reports alone. Fare perception was one of them.

Through field engagement and direct driver conversations, it became clear that fare levels strongly influenced willingness to accept trips, especially in high-traffic zones where opportunity cost was high. Although the data showed declining activity, it did not directly encode how drivers felt about earnings relative to effort.

By correlating fare structures with trip acceptance patterns and validating these insights through on-the-ground interactions, a data-informed decision was made to adjust fares strategically. When fares were reduced in a targeted and controlled manner, ride volume increased, acceptance rates improved, and overall marketplace liquidity strengthened.

Key Insight:
Data guided the decision, but real-world dependencies completed the picture.

Designing a Data-Guided Field Strategy

Armed with these insights, the project shifted away from generic acquisition tactics toward a focused field strategy. The objective was not volume, but precision. Drivers were segmented into active, semi-active, and inactive cohorts, allowing interventions to be tailored rather than broadcast.

Geographic heatmaps highlighted underserved yet high-demand locations, enabling teams to prioritize zones with the highest potential impact.

Execution: Mobility Over Static Operations

One of the most consequential decisions was replacing static registration tents with a mobile field operation. Teams moved through transport terminals, fuel stations, and high-traffic driver zones instead of waiting for drivers to come to them.

This approach reduced friction, increased visibility, and allowed for direct conversations with drivers who had previously disengaged due to poor experiences or eroding trust.

Key Insight:
Data-driven execution is most powerful when it meets people where they already are.

Results and Measurable Impact

The intervention delivered measurable improvements within a short timeframe. By aligning supply with demand and rebuilding driver confidence, the marketplace began to stabilize.

  • Higher driver reactivation and onboarding rates
  • Reduced ETAs in previously underserved zones
  • Improved trip acceptance and completion rates
  • Stronger rider confidence and platform reliability

More importantly, the project demonstrated that sustainable growth requires more than dashboards. It requires decisions shaped by data and validated through execution.

Key Lessons Learned

  • Data should guide decisions, not replace judgment
  • Inactive users often represent hidden growth potential
  • Field execution is strongest when informed by evidence
  • Trust is as critical to marketplaces as incentives

Conclusion

Reviving ride demand in Tema was not about doing more. It was about doing differently. By combining data analysis, fare experimentation, field insight, and on-the-ground execution, this project transformed a long-standing marketplace imbalance into a growth-ready system.

When data is treated as a decision-making compass rather than a reporting artifact, it stops describing the past and starts shaping the future.
